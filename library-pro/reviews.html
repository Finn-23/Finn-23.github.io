Rutgers’ Student Looking for a Quiet Study Spot? – Try the Third Floor of the LSM

By Finn Butler | Published 29 March 2026

Rating: 4/5

  • Campus: Busch
  • Address: 165 Bevier Road, Piscataway, NJ 08854-8009
  • Public hours: 8am-7pm Monday-Thursday | 8am-10pm Friday | 1pm-6pm Saturday | 2:30pm-10pm Sunday
  • Student hours: 8am-12am Monday-Thursday | 8am-10pm Friday | 1pm-6pm Saturday | 2:30pm-10pm Sunday

The third floor of the Library of Science and Medicine has been denoted the “quiet floor.” As I aim to map and document the uncharted, daily, environment of this library from a boots-on-the-ground perspective, I decided the best way to understand the ecosystem of the third floor was to spend the day there.

After climbing up the three flights of stairs to the third floor, a sign on the door greets you with the phrase “Please, no talking on this floor; Quiet study only.” I discovered that this sign is posted all around the third floor, in case one forgets, which, I observed, happening more often than you may think at a college where all the students are presumably literate.

Over my time on the third floor, I recorded a number of things that I liked and found positive about the floor. For example, I felt that the temperature was well maintained, being neither too hot nor too cold like the way some other Rutgers’ buildings are prone to being. I also liked that, for the most part, it is pin-drop quiet; I heard only a few small conversations breaking the silence throughout the duration of my stay. I also found, on the day I went, that the floor was not busy, and I had no problem finding a place to study. Whether this changes throughout the semester, during times like finals week, will require further observation. The desks are wide, so I can sprawl out the contents of my backpack. There is a large window in the front of the library. I enjoyed being able to see the sun on the brisk, spring day I decided to carry out this study.

The “quiet floor” is not without its negatives. The view itself provided by the window--a muddy, nearly tree-less field of Busch campus--leaves much to be desired, especially compared to the views provided from other buildings, particularly on the College Ave and Cook/Douglass campus. I can also imagine one might want more group study rooms on the floor, as there are only about 3, though in my opinion there should be none, as it is, after all, the “quiet floor,” and sound often travels out of these rooms. I think these already existing study rooms could benefit from some soundproofing, to ensure that the “quiet floor” is kept quiet. I am also not a fan of the florescent lights that shine throughout the entire floor.

If you, like me, find yourself studying often, and require silence to focus, the third floor of the Library of Science and Medicine is for you. It is rarely full, the climate is well controlled, and the location is convenient if you are a STEM student, who has many classes on the Busch campus.

Product Review

Library Ear Plugs: Comfortable Silence, Offered on Demand

By Finn Butler | Published 29 March 2026

Rating: 3/5

  • Available: Circulation Desk
  • Price: Free
  • Color: Orange
  • Reusability: Disposable

Throughout the vast and varied landscape of the Library of Science and Medicine, I have observed students planting themselves at all tables, splaying themselves across every couch and beanbag, and tucking themselves away in every nook and cranny. They come here for numerous reasons: to study (or to carry out an act resembling studying, but actually involves more TikTok scrolling than page-flipping), to use the computers, or to relax in-between classes. No doubt, one of the most popular reasons for student migration to this library is to seek quiet refuge from the anxious-air of busy bus stops or their often-tumultuous dormitories. Though quiet in this library is not always guaranteed. One often overhears the debating of how work will be divided in a group project, what information friends plan to triage from their review as they franticly cram before an exam, or last weekend’s drama, told in extraordinary, visceral detail. The Busch library, however, offers a solution to this: silence on demand. This is provided to students in the form of bright orange earplugs. These earplugs are available upon request, at no cost, from the main desk on the first floor, just to the left of the main entrance. Given that they’re free, I decided to take advantage of this offer.

The ear plugs are cone-shaped, and made of a semi-rigid foam, allowing them to easily be inserted into the ear canal. I wore the earplugs for the entire duration that I was in the library between classes—about 2 hours—and did not experience any discomfort. As mentioned, the ear plugs are a bright, hazardous orange. The shade is very similar to that of a traffic cone. While this does makes them hard to match with an outfit, the color seems to serve a different purpose. The bright color sends a signal to the students around you: be quiet, don’t talk to me.

As the ear plugs are offered for free, one might expect that they are not the highest quality. I held this expectation and found that it was an expectation met by my experience. The plugs do limit the sound you hear, but they do not cancel it out. Muffled conversation still makes its way through. The words that come through are not discernable, but nonetheless potentially distracting. I also found that the plugs themselves began to experience some degradation, just from my placement and removal of them. Tiny bits of orange foam began to come off on my fingers and, I can only assume, my ears as well. I would definitely not plan on reusing them for another study session. Good thing they’re free!

Overall, if you are someone who likes to study in silence, I would recommend grabbing a pair of these ear plugs from central circulation the next time you’re in the library. They’re free and easily obtained and they do block out sound to a certain extent. I find they work best as a complement to the third floor, blocking out any minor sounds that one could find distracting. I doubt the plugs’ utility on the first and second floors, as they are usually rife with conversation—conversation that the plugs probably cannot drown out.
